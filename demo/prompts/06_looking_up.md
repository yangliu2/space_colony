# 👀 Looking Up from Surface — The Iconic O'Neill View

## Prompt

Photorealistic upward-looking view from the ground surface inside a rotating O'Neill cylinder habitat. The viewer lies on their back on green grass, looking straight up. This is the most iconic and psychologically striking view in the habitat.

Directly overhead, 1,964 meters above, the opposite land strip is clearly visible — an inverted landscape with miniature trees, buildings, pathways, and a small lake, all appearing to hang upside-down from the "ceiling." The details are visible but small at nearly 2 km distance, similar to looking at a landscape from a high-altitude airplane. The opposite surface is lit by the same reflected sunlight, showing the same warm golden tones.

Between the viewer's land strip and the one overhead, two window strips are visible — transparent bands approximately 1 kilometer wide, showing black space with sharp white stars. The window strips curve from the left and right edges of the land strip, wrapping upward and over to frame the opposite land surface. Through the windows, the back faces of the external mirrors may be partially visible — flexible reflective surfaces extending diagonally from the anti-sun end of the cylinder. From inside, you see their dark thermal-coated back faces, not the reflective side.

The third land strip is visible at the sides, curving upward at steep angles — the landscape appears to climb vertically and then continue overhead, creating a continuous tube of inhabited surface broken only by the three sky-like window bands.

Scattered clouds float at perhaps 200-300 meters altitude, well below the center of the cylinder. They are small cumulus-type clouds, formed by the habitat's managed weather system. They partially obscure the view of the opposite side.

The overall sensation is of standing inside an enormous illuminated tube — a world turned inside-out, where "up" leads to more ground rather than open sky.

Style: photorealistic, dramatic upward perspective (lying on back looking straight up), wide-angle or fisheye lens, lush green foreground grass framing the view, clouds at mid-altitude, the opposite landscape sharp enough to see buildings. This is the money shot — the view that defines the O'Neill cylinder experience.

## Key Physics Details for Accuracy

- The opposite side is 1,964 meters away (2× radius)
- Three land strips + three window strips wrap the full 360°
- "Up" does NOT mean away from gravity — gravity pulls you toward the rim everywhere
- Clouds form below the axis but well above the surface (convection in rotating frame)
- The Coriolis effect deflects weather patterns — winds curve
- Rain falls "straight down" (toward the rim) locally, but the overall circulation is complex
- At night (mirrors closed), the windows show only stars — three bands of starfield
- The opposite land strip is upside-down relative to you, but its residents feel right-side-up
