## Known Issues with Current Image

1. **Mirror not long enough.** The mirror visible through the window looks like

   vast rectangular sheet running from the anti-sun end to roughly the sun-
   facing end, visible through the entire length of the window strip.
2. **Six-strip structure not visible.** Looking up from a land strip, you should

   3 land strips and 3 window strips clearly distinguishable overhead. The
   current image looks like a single large opening rather than one of three
   60° window strips bounded by land strips on each side.
3. **Light source direction unclear.** The sunlight should clearly be entering
   through ONE specific window strip — a distinct band of light from one
   direction, not ambient light from an undefined opening above. The two
   adjacent land strips (curving overhead at 60° on each side of the window)
   should frame the light source.

## Prompt

Photorealistic interior view of an O'Neill cylinder habitat during simulated
sunrise, standing on one of the three land strips looking up toward the
nearest window strip.

**The cylinder interior has six alternating 60° strips running its full 2 km
length: three opaque land strips and three transparent window strips.** From
this viewpoint on a land strip, the landscape curves steeply upward on both
sides. To the left, the land curves up to meet a window strip (transparent,
showing black space and mirror beyond). To the right, the land curves up to
meet the other adjacent window strip. Directly overhead (roughly 120° away on
the far side of the cylinder) the opposite land strip is visible, curving
across the "ceiling" with its own vegetation and buildings seen from below.
The six-strip structure — land, window, land, window, land, window — should
be clearly distinguishable as you look around the interior.

**One of the three mirrors is opening.** Through the nearest window strip,
an enormous mirror is visible — a flat, rectangular polished aluminum surface
that runs the **full 2 km length** of the cylinder. It is hinged at the
anti-sun end and extends diagonally toward the sun end. At this moment it is
tilted at about 30° from the hull, catching axial sunlight and redirecting it
radially inward through the window strip. The mirror fills most of the view
through the window — it is as wide as the window strip and stretches into the
distance along the cylinder's full length, its far end vanishing into
perspective 2 km away.

Warm golden light streams in through this one window strip at a clear angle,
casting long dramatic shadows across the land strip. The light enters from a
**specific direction** — from the window strip to the left (or right), not
from directly overhead. The land strip nearest to the opening mirror is
illuminated first — grass, trees, and building facades catch the warm light
while the opposite side remains in pre-dawn dimness.

The window strip frames the view: fused quartz panels in thin steel cable
mesh, visible as a subtle grid. Through the glass, the mirror's gleaming
reflective surface and the black void of space are visible beyond. The sun
itself is not directly visible — only its reflection in the mirror.

Inside the habitat, dew on grass catches the light, street lights along
pathways dim automatically. The opposite side of the cylinder (nearly 2 km
overhead) is still partially dark — its corresponding mirror hasn't opened
yet, creating asymmetric lighting.

Style: photorealistic golden hour photography, dramatic directional lighting,
lens flare through window panels, warm color palette, cinematic composition
with foreground grass/dew detail and background cylinder curvature showing
the full six-strip structure. The beauty is the engineered sunrise — nature
simulated by mirrors and glass at massive scale.

## Key Physics Details for Accuracy

- **Six strips:** The interior has exactly 6 alternating strips (3 land, 3
  window), each spanning 60° of the circumference. This structure must be
  visible — looking up, you see land curving up, then a window strip, then
  more land, etc.
- **Mirror runs the full cylinder length (2 km).** It is NOT a short panel.
  Through the window, the mirror should dominate the view, stretching to a
  vanishing point 2 km away.
- **Light enters from ONE window strip** at a clear directional angle — not
  from a vague overhead opening. The two land strips on either side of the
  window visually frame the light source.
- Sunrise is MECHANICAL — mirrors physically open on hinges, not a sun rising
  over a horizon
- The "sun" is a reflection — directional but not a point source (mirror is
  ~1 km wide)
- Dawn happens simultaneously along the full 2 km length (mirror opens along
  its full length)
- Different mirrors can open at different rates — the habitat operator
  controls sunrise timing
- Light entering through one window illuminates parts of all three land strips
- The color temperature is slightly different from Earth sunrise — no
  atmospheric reddening, but the mirror angle creates oblique reflection that
  warms the color
- The rotation means the "sunrise direction" changes relative to land features
  once per minute — shadows sweep around
